**The surveillance state isn’t coming. It’s already in your pocket, and you gave it permission when you clicked 'accept cookies.'**

Byron Tau’s *Means of Control* isn’t just a book. It’s a grim little map of the world we already live in, where the government doesn’t need to spy on you directly — it just buys the data from a broker who scraped it off your phone while you were tracking your steps or checking the weather.

I didn’t need this book to *believe* we live in a surveillance state. I’ve been surveilled. When I was organizing against pipelines in British Columbia, I was one of many activists who discovered, through FOI requests, that the RCMP and CSIS were monitoring us. We weren’t even being sneaky. We were in the streets, on camera, on record — the kind of “opposition” that’s supposed to be protected in a democracy. But they spied on us anyway, and then tried to justify it under the usual banner of "security."

So yeah, I didn’t read *Means of Control* hoping for a surprise. I read it hoping for clarity. And I got it.

> “It is now possible to build a government surveillance apparatus without passing a law or writing a regulation.” — Byron Tau

This is the core of the book. Surveillance has gone freelance. All the government had to do was let private companies build the infrastructure, and then buy access to it.

Tau walks us through this like a good investigative reporter should: with receipts. The story about lawmakers dragging intel officials into secure rooms to show them they were being tracked visiting gay bars? That should’ve been on the front page for weeks. Instead, it’s a footnote in an industry that treats your location like a free sample.

**Ad tech isn’t just annoying. It’s surveillance with a user-friendly dashboard.**

And no, the U.S. doesn’t get to act like this is somehow better than China. Tau makes the case that America’s surveillance ecosystem is actually harder to challenge, precisely because it’s fragmented, privatized, and hidden behind UX.

What Tau describes feels like a natural sequel to the work of others who’ve been sounding the alarm:

- Surveillance Valley by Yasha Levine — https://surveillancevalley.com  
- Your Face Belongs to Us by Kashmir Hill — https://www.kashmirhill.com/your-face-belongs-to-us  
- Privacy is Power by Carissa Véliz — https://carissaveliz.com/privacy-is-power/  
- Chasing Shadows by Ronald Deibert — https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Chasing-Shadows/Ronald-J-Deibert/9781668014042

Tau doesn’t sensationalize anything. He just shows you how it works. Who buys the data. Who sells it. Who gets caught in the crosshairs.

If you care about abortion access, about trans rights, about Indigenous sovereignty, or about just walking through the world without being followed by invisible advertisers and federal agencies — this is required reading.
